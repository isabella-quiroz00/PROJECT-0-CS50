WHAT HAPPENS IN OUR BRAIN WHEN WE EAT?

The food that is ingested travels all the way down the gastrointestinal tract. Once in the intestine most biomolecules are broken down so that the process of absorption can take place and the nutrients from food can get to the blood, and furthermore to all the body. Carbohydrates, for example, provide energy to the muscles and primary to the brain. However, carbs may also activate some systems of response in the nervous system that control hunger and satiety.

CARBOHYDRATES

  1. Gastrointestinal tract
  2. Nervous reward systems
  3. Addiction

Gastrointestinal tract

There are two principal monosaccharide carbohydrates: glucose and fructose. And, even though both can provide energy to the brain, glucose is more abundant and is brain’s main source of ATP.

Within the digestive system carbs are broken down into smaller molecules, so that in the intestine they can be absorbed. This process occurs when glucose and fructose cross the intestinal membrane throughout protein channels (GLUT) into the blood.

Then, blood transports glucose through the blood brain barrier (BBB) and enter the nervous system with astrocytes’ help. This way glucose transforms into energy that is used directly by neurons.

Nervous reward systems

In the brain, the region responsible of dopamine secretion is called the ventral tegmental area. This region controls the reward pathways within the nervous system and furthermore the behavior of people when eating some specific food.

However, this system is regulated by the secretion of three principal substances. Insulin and leptin activate satiety and reduce people’s urge to eat. In the contrary, ghrelin induce hunger.

Addiction

Food that is highly energetic and palatable activates reward pathways within the nervous system. This way ventral tegmental area secretes dopamine to nucleus accumbens, which sends a signal to the hippocampus, so that people can remember the experience of eating some specific food; and also, to the prefrontal cortex, that generates a responsive action to the previous stimuli of eating.

Eating disorders such as anorexia, bulimia and obesity are examples of how reward systems modify and collapse, when the function of multiple regions in the brain alter and generate eating behaviors such as eating too much or not eating at all. These happen because food doesn’t generate the same pleasure as it normally should.
